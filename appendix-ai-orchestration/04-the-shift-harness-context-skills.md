# A4 — The Shift: Harness, Context, and Skills

Everything in A1–A3 is real, running software, verified directly. This module is a different kind of claim — not "here's how a tool works," but "here's what actually happened to this whole category of tool, this year, with real numbers behind it." Every specific figure below was checked against a live web search run the day this was written, not carried over from training data or repeated uncritically from the observation that prompted this appendix — sources are listed in `99-resources.md`.

## Two different audiences, two different tools, 2025–2026

**The no-code/vibe-coding wave** — platforms like Lovable and Replit — brought a real, distinct audience into building software: people who don't write code at all, describing what they want in plain language and getting a working app back. Genuinely useful, genuinely different from what follows.

**The developer-facing agentic harness** is a separate, parallel story, and the 2026 adoption data is specific rather than vague: Claude Code and Cursor together account for over half of primary-tool selections among developers surveyed in 2026 (28% and 24% respectively), and most professional developers who use either one run *both* — Cursor plus Claude Code together is the single most common real-world stack, not an either/or choice. Claude Code itself is described as the fastest-growing developer product ever measured — zero to a $2.5B annualized run-rate in nine months, with roughly 6× adoption growth between April 2025 and January 2026 — while Cursor's own growth (from $1M to $2B in annual recurring revenue in about 28 months) is separately described as the fastest a SaaS company has ever grown. Among senior developers specifically, 46% name Claude Code their most-loved tool, against 19% for Cursor and 9% for GitHub Copilot — even though Copilot still has the largest installed base overall. OpenAI's Codex occupies a genuinely different position again: a cloud-first, asynchronous agent for teams that want to assign batches of work and review the results, rather than a tool run interactively in a terminal.

Google's own entry, **Antigravity**, launched as an agent-first IDE (built as a fork of VS Code) in November 2025 alongside Gemini 3 — and by its 2.0 release at Google I/O 2026, the product's own center of gravity had shifted: less "a code editor with AI added," more a platform for running and managing *teams* of agents in parallel, with a desktop app, CLI, and SDK. The direction of travel across all of these tools, independently, is the same: less "assist a human typing code," more "supervise agents that do the typing."

## The other half of 2025–2026: a "limited cousin," gone viral

A materially different kind of tool captured public attention in the same window, for a different reason. **OpenClaw** — a self-hosted, open-source personal AI agent, genuinely a "limited cousin" of a full coding harness in the precise sense that matters here: it wires an LLM's tool-calling ability to real execution surfaces (shell commands, file access, browser automation, Docker) and to consumer messaging platforms directly — Telegram, Discord, WhatsApp, Slack, iMessage — rather than a developer's terminal. It genuinely can write new skills for itself autonomously and maintain long-term memory of a user's own preferences. It launched in November 2025 under the name Clawdbot, renamed to Moltbot in January 2026 after a trademark dispute, and rebranded a final time to OpenClaw on January 29, 2026 — accumulating well over 100,000 GitHub stars within weeks of its initial viral spread. This is exactly the "excited the populace" half of the story: not developers adopting a professional tool, but a much wider audience running an autonomous agent connected to their own messaging apps and personal files.

That popularity had a real, physical, verifiable consequence. Running a capable local model, rather than paying per-call for a hosted one, became genuinely attractive at OpenClaw's own scale of adoption — and Apple's own Mac mini and Mac Studio lines, whose unified memory architecture is unusually well-suited to local inference, hit a real supply crunch as a direct result. By mid-2026, ordinary Mac mini wait times had stretched from around three weeks to nearly three months, with higher-memory configurations (64GB) specifically quoted at 16–18 weeks; the $599 base configuration was reported sold out and effectively discontinued. Underneath that: global DRAM and NAND flash prices rose 40–60% from early 2025 levels, driven overwhelmingly by AI infrastructure demand — and AI server memory alone consumed roughly 30% of total global DRAM production in the first quarter of 2026, up from about 8% in 2023. This is, genuinely, the first time consumer-level AI software demand has visibly dented a hardware product line's own availability — not a data-center story alone, a laptop-and-desktop-buyer story too, and OpenClaw's own viral growth is named directly as a real contributing cause in reporting on the shortage.

## The guardrails catch up: Hermes, and OpenClaw's own ecosystem of forks

OpenClaw's early, viral growth outpaced its own safety defaults — permissive settings that were reasonable for one person's own laptop became genuinely dangerous once people started running the same software on public, internet-exposed servers. That gap produced a real, direct response: **Hermes Agent**, positioned explicitly as the safer-by-default alternative — permission prompts, command review, and careful secret handling shipping *on* by default, rather than left for the operator to configure correctly themselves. As of the sources checked for this appendix, Hermes had zero reported agent-specific CVEs — a fact worth stating precisely rather than as a settled verdict: that reflects a newer, smaller-exposure project so far, not independently proven hardening, and both projects had since added sandboxing options and approval flows of their own.

OpenClaw itself spawned a genuine ecosystem rather than staying a single project: NVIDIA's own NemoClaw variant adds its own guardrail layer; NanoClaw adds real container isolation; ClawHub adds third-party skill scanning. A cluster of much smaller, lighter forks — nanoclaw, zeroclaw, ironclaw, picoclaw — circulate with real interest but limited production track records; a genuinely notable one, Nanobot (out of the University of Hong Kong), reimplements roughly 80% of OpenClaw's own core functionality in about 4,000 lines of Python — small enough, its own advocates note, to read and audit in a single afternoon, a direct answer to the "can I actually trust what this autonomous thing is doing" question a much larger codebase makes harder to answer honestly.

## What this appendix's own methodology already demonstrated

Here's the actual pivot, and it isn't abstract — every guide in this 28-guide series, including this appendix, was built the way this section describes rather than the way A1–A3 describe. No LangChain chain orchestrated the DesignCurriculum → verify → WriteSession loop this series ran, guide after guide. No LangGraph state machine decided when a curriculum architecture needed revision before writing began. No n8n workflow triggered a toolchain install and a verification pass. Every one of those steps happened inside a single harness — persistent context across an entire guide's construction, real tool use (a shell, a file editor, a package manager), and a reusable **Skill** (this project's own `StudyGuide` skill, invoked identically for Fortran and for Rails) — the same three words this module's own title names.

This isn't a claim that LangChain, LangGraph, and n8n became pointless — A1–A3's own verified material stands on its own, and the real tradeoffs named throughout this appendix are genuine: n8n's visual builder is a real, right tool for a non-programmer who needs to see and edit a workflow's own shape directly; LangGraph's explicit, inspectable state graph is a real, right tool for a workflow that specifically needs a human approval step visible and auditable at a named point, not just embedded implicitly in an agent's own reasoning. What actually happened is narrower and more precise than "orchestration frameworks got replaced": a large share of what those frameworks existed to hand-build — memory across a long task, deciding which tool to call next, retrying sensibly on failure — is, for a working developer specifically, now something a sufficiently capable model *plus* a real harness does directly, without a separate orchestration layer being assembled by hand for every new project. The frameworks this appendix's own A1–A3 modules verified didn't get worse; the ground they were built to cover got smaller, precisely at the developer-tooling layer, precisely because the model-plus-harness combination this whole book was written inside of got better at doing that job itself.
